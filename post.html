Why We Switched to Bayesian A/B Testing

Mar 15, 2025 · 12 min read · Nikita Boyarkin

In 2024, our team ran 127 experiments. 34 of them showed early significance, only to reverse once we reached the planned sample size. The cost: delayed launches, frustrated stakeholders, and a creeping distrust in data.

Peeking at p-values is an occupational hazard. When product managers can see a dashboard updating in real time, the temptation to call a winner early is immense. And who can blame them? In a fast-moving startup, "wait two more weeks" sounds like a luxury.

The Problem with Peeking

The classical frequentist approach assumes a fixed sample size decided before the experiment starts. Every time you look at the results before reaching that sample size, you're inflating your Type I error rate. Do it enough, and your 5% false positive rate becomes 20%, 30%, or worse.

We tried sequential testing with O'Brien-Fleming boundaries. It helped, but the math was opaque to stakeholders. "Why is the boundary so wide early on?" became a recurring question in experiment review meetings.

The Bayesian Alternative

Instead of asking "is there an effect?", Bayesian inference asks "how likely is it that the effect is positive?" This subtle reframing changes everything about how you communicate results to product managers.

The key metric becomes the Probability of Direction (PoD): the probability that the treatment is better than control. If PoD > 95%, you can be reasonably confident. If it's 80%, maybe wait. The metric is intuitive and doesn't require a statistics degree to understand.

import pymc as pm

with pm.Model() as model:
    # Priors based on historical experiment data
    mu_control = pm.Normal("mu_control", mu=0.10, sigma=0.02)
    mu_treatment = pm.Normal("mu_treatment", mu=0.10, sigma=0.02)

    # Likelihood
    obs_control = pm.Binomial(
        "obs_control",
        n=control_users,
        p=mu_control,
        observed=control_conversions
    )
    obs_treatment = pm.Binomial(
        "obs_treatment",
        n=treatment_users,
        p=mu_treatment,
        observed=treatment_conversions
    )

    # Trace
    trace = pm.sample(2000)

Results After 6 Months

  • Experiment duration reduced by 15% on average (we stopped over-running)
  • Stakeholder confidence in results increased (qualitative survey)
  • Zero cases of "early significance reversal" because we no longer rely on arbitrary thresholds
  • One surprise: we started running experiments we previously thought were underpowered

When Frequentist Still Wins

I'm not suggesting Bayesian is always better. For regulatory reporting, frequentist p-values are still the standard. For very small sample sizes, priors can dominate the result in uncomfortable ways. And if your team doesn't understand priors, you might replace one opaque method with another.

The switch worked for us because:

  1. We had enough historical data to build informed priors
  2. Our stakeholders were data-literate and open to new frameworks
  3. We kept rigorous pre-registration — the Bayesian approach answered "should we ship?" not "what should we test?"
